Living Building:
Growing Tropical Architecture Through Grafting and Inosculation

Summary

My thesis explores a new form of ecological construction in that building components are planted, grown, and harvested. Grafting is explored as the primary method for constructing buildings with living trees. Referencing Baubotanik, an ongoing research on living plant construction at the Technical University Munich led by Prof Ferdinand Ludwig, grafting can be used to merge many trees into one single organism that is load-bearing, durable and suitable for architecture. My thesis explores this new method of construction further and applies it in the context of the Tropics, where trees grow much faster due to the abundant sunlight and warm temperatures throughout the year.

My thesis work includes research on the principles of living tree construction, specifically on the nature and growth patterns of Ficus Elastica, a tropical tree that I chose for its unique properties as the primary material for my investigation. My thesis project proposes three key building components: (1) a column system, (2) a facade system and (3) a floor system. These three systems demonstrate the great potential of living tree construction for future architecture, from planting and growing to harvesting and living.

Baubotanik: A research group based in Germany that uses the method of grafting to construct buildings out of living trees.
Grafting: A horticultural technique to join two plants into one.
Inosculation: A natural phenomenon where trunks, branches or roots fuse together.
Ficus Elastica: A type of strangler fig that is commonly found throughout the tropical region.

Column System

The column system comprises of a metal scaffolding structure with plants growing around it. Plants on level 1 will be rooted onto the ground, while plants above ground will have their roots tucked into a special floor slab that consists of soil spaces. A diagrid lattice is used for the scaffolding structure for optimal support with minimal amount of materials required. The column will be hollow to act as a light shaft, allowing natural light into the building for both the occupants and the plants.

Once the height reaches above 75m, it is not efficient to increase the diameter of column further.

Facade System

The facade system comprises of a metal scaffolding structure with plants growing around it. Density of plants can vary across the building to create multiple different atmospheres for the interior space. Using plants as the facade will also bring many ecosystem services to the occupants, such as lowered temperature and improved air quality. A diagrid scaffolding is used for the facade to have load-bearing properties. This new facade system provides an alternative technique to implementing vertical greenery onto buildings.
